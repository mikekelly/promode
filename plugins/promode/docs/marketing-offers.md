# Marketing offers: designing the thing, not the page

Routed mechanics doc — the `senior-marketer` def directs a read of this file when a dispatch involves designing or diagnosing an offer: guarantees, bonuses, scarcity, naming, packaging, or a stuck-offer rescue. Cold-readable and opinion-dense; it wraps frameworks the pinned model already knows with promode's *when/which/why*, and spells out only the mechanics the model half-knows.

Provenance: distilled 2026-07-15 from the `offers` skill of coreyhaines31/marketingskills (MIT, Corey Haines), reference files `value-equation.md`, `guarantee-design.md`, `bonus-stacking.md`, `scarcity-urgency.md`, `offer-formats.md`, `offer-anatomy.md`, `examples.md`. Mining ≠ endorsing: quantitative claims below are the repo's, unverified.

<the-offer-is-the-thing>
The governing diagnostic: **the offer is the thing, not the page.** Most "we need better copy" requests are "we need a better offer" requests in disguise — a stronger offer with average copy converts immediately; better copy on a weak offer compounds slowly. Before polishing expression, improve the offer. And when you do improve it, **project lift honestly**: a single-component offer change delivers ~10–40%, stacked iterations 2–3× — anyone promising 5× is selling something. (Numbers are the repo's; the stance is the point — calibrate expectations against hype.)

**Route by business type first.** Offer construction — bonuses, guarantees, scarcity, naming — is for services, courses, coaching, high-ticket, and direct-response. In **pure self-serve SaaS the levers are different**: tier structure, value metric, and packaging, not bonus stacks. If the work is self-serve SaaS pricing, that's a product/pricing call (route to the CMO/CPO/CTO boundary), not offer construction.
</the-offer-is-the-thing>

<value-equation-diagnosis>
Diagnose a stuck offer with the **value equation** (Hormozi — the model knows the four levers: dream outcome, perceived likelihood of achievement, time delay, effort & sacrifice). The opinions the model won't volunteer:

- **Score the four levers; the lowest is the binding constraint.** Fix **one lever per iteration** — stacking changes hides which one moved the needle.
- **"Lower the price" is almost never the move.** Price is the *comparison*, not the value — treat the urge to cut price as "raise the numerator or lower the denominator" instead. Payment *structure* is its own lever: often add a payment plan rather than lowering the number.
- **Stuck offers usually need more proof, not more features** — and **build proof before the guarantee**: strong proof + weak guarantee beats the reverse. Reach for a guarantee to *reverse residual risk*, not to substitute for evidence you haven't gathered.
</value-equation-diagnosis>

<guarantee-selection>
Guarantees are not one-size-fits-all, and **the wrong guarantee hurts more than none** (it signals the wrong thing to the wrong buyer). The model knows guarantee *patterns* (money-back, conditional/performance, better-than-money-back, anti-guarantee) but not this selection discipline — spelled out because a cold probe confirmed the model reconstructs the patterns but not the taxonomy/tree.

**Pick the guarantee from business model + buyer sophistication.** An 8-type taxonomy sits behind a decision tree; the load-bearing rules:
- **Match to the buyer's dominant emotion.** Cold, low-trust, low-sophistication markets → maximize risk reversal (plain, bold, unconditional). Sophisticated, hype-skeptical markets → an over-the-top guarantee *backfires* (pattern-matches to a scam); a specific conditional/performance guarantee or a structural/implied one (shared stake) reads as more credible.
- **Two conditions maximum.** Every condition you add is friction the honest buyer also pays; past two, the guarantee stops reassuring and starts filtering out good buyers.
- **Stress-test at ~10% invocation.** Before you ship a guarantee, ask: *if 10% of buyers invoked this, does the offer still survive?* If the honest failure rate plus fraud would sink you at 10%, the guarantee is too generous for this delivery cost — step down the type or add a (single) condition.
- **Anti-guarantees fit premium / self-qualified buyers.** A bold "all sales final, this is only for people who are serious" repels low-commitment buyers and signals status — but only where the audience reads it as confidence, not risk-dumping. Poor fit for cold traffic, commodities, impulse buys.

Upstream reference (currency-check before acting — perishable specifics): `https://raw.githubusercontent.com/coreyhaines31/marketingskills/main/skills/offers/references/guarantee-design.md`.
</guarantee-selection>

<bonuses-and-scarcity>
**Every bonus closes one specific named objection** (speed / trust / stuck / a decision-pattern), and bonuses are *additive to a complete core* — if the offer doesn't convert without the core, fix the core; a bonus can't rescue a broken offer, and inflating the stack past a working core actively hurts (a buyer who gets everything up front is more likely to abandon). **Stagger delivery** — the *completion* of the offer is itself a marketing asset.

**Scarcity is the close, not the offer.** Fix the value equation first; if you're tempted to *fake* scarcity, you have a value-equation problem, not a scarcity problem. Real scarcity is buyer-verifiable and actually enforced. **Skip scarcity entirely** on subscriptions, premium, and high-trust offers — faked or forced urgency there spends the trust that is the compounding asset.
</bonuses-and-scarcity>

<packaging-and-format>
- **Productize services into named, fixed-scope offers** ("The 8-Week X"). Unnamed services compete on price; named ones compete on positioning. Test the name with: *can a buyer text a friend about it?*
- **Match offer format to business type** — the biggest offer mistake is borrowing across types (dropping direct-response bonus-stacking onto premium B2B). For **enterprise B2B, time-to-value IS the offer**; for **cohort coaching, the room IS the offer**. Don't contaminate one type's playbook with another's.
</packaging-and-format>
